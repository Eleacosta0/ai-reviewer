# AI-Reviewer Rubric Definitions

All scoring definitions for the 5 design styles and the Universal Rubric. Scores: 1 (Broken) · 2 (Weak) · 3 (Acceptable) · 4 (Strong) · 5 (Canva Excellence)

---

## 01\. Editorial / Fashion Photography

*High-contrast, aspirational visual language borrowed from fashion magazines. Relies on strong imagery, restrained copy, and typographic hierarchy to create a sense of prestige and intentionality.*

### Rubric 1: Image & Copy Integration

| Score | Rating | Description |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| 1 | Broken | Text is pasted on top of the image with no spatial logic. Readability is destroyed by competing visual elements. There is no relationship between the image and the words — they feel like two separate documents merged by accident. |
| 2 | Weak | Some attempt to position text near the image, but the copy fights with busy areas of the photo. Overlaps obscure key faces or focal points. The overall composition feels crowded and unresolved. |
| 3 | Acceptable | Text is placed in a relatively clear area of the image, avoiding the worst conflicts. Basic hierarchy exists. However, the placement feels default rather than deliberate — no creative tension or intentional dialogue between image and copy. |
| 4 | Strong | Typography is clearly positioned to complement the image's negative space or focal point. The design shows awareness of the photograph's composition, whether single or multi-image. Copy and image reinforce each other's message without competing. |
| 5 | Canva Excellence | Image and copy form a unified visual statement. Typography is woven into the spatial logic of the photograph — perhaps echoing a line, interacting with a figure, or using the image's own rhythm. Multi-image compositions, unconventional type placement (vertical, rotated, architectural), and deliberate cropping are all valid editorial tools when used with spatial intention. The design could not work without both elements. |

### Rubric 2: Typography & Typographic Hierarchy

| Score | Rating | Description |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| 1 | Broken | Multiple fonts are mixed with no logic. Sizes are random — subheadings may be larger than the headline. The eye has no natural reading path. The design looks assembled from mismatched components. |
| 2 | Weak | A rough hierarchy exists but is undermined by inconsistent weights, poor spacing, or a font choice that conflicts with the editorial mood. The typographic palette feels accidental rather than curated. |
| 3 | Acceptable | Headline, subheading, and body text are distinguishable. Font pairing is functional if generic. Letter-spacing and leading are adequate. Lacks the editorial character that would make the design memorable. |
| 4 | Strong | Font selection is deliberate and appropriate for the fashion/editorial context (e.g., a refined serif headline with a clean sans-serif body). Hierarchy guides the eye smoothly. Tracking and leading are calibrated with care. |
| 5 | Canva Excellence | Typography is a design feature in itself. Contrast between weights, scales, or styles creates visual drama without sacrificing clarity. The typeface choice feels specific to the brand voice — it could not be swapped without changing the meaning of the piece. |

### Rubric 3: White Space & Breathing Room

| Score | Rating | Description |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| 1 | Broken | The canvas is packed to the edges. Every available pixel is filled with elements. No margins, no internal spacing, no room for the eye to rest. The design produces visual anxiety. |
| 2 | Weak | Some margin exists but it is uneven or insufficient. Elements are too tightly grouped. The design attempts restraint but the instinct to fill space wins, resulting in a cluttered feel. |
| 3 | Acceptable | Margins are present and consistent. Elements have adequate breathing room. The composition doesn't feel overcrowded but also doesn't leverage white space as a deliberate design tool. |
| 4 | Strong | White space is actively used to direct attention and create pacing. Generous margins amplify the prestige of the design. The emptiness communicates as much as the content. |
| 5 | Canva Excellence | Negative space is the visual center of gravity. The editorial luxury of 'less is more' is fully realized. Space makes the design feel expensive, confident, and intentional — a hallmark of top-tier fashion publication design. |

### Rubric 4: Color Restraint & Tonal Control

| Score | Rating | Description |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| 1 | Broken | Colors are chosen randomly with no relationship to the image palette or brand. Multiple saturated colors compete simultaneously. The palette undermines the aspirational quality the style demands. |
| 2 | Weak | A dominant color is attempted but secondary choices introduce noise. The palette is not derived from the image, creating a disconnect. Contrast between text color and background is insufficient in places. |
| 3 | Acceptable | Color palette is controlled — typically two to three colors. Text contrast is adequate. The palette is not disruptive, though it may feel generic rather than specifically curated. |
| 4 | Strong | Palette is pulled from or harmonized with the key image. Achromatic or near-achromatic palettes are expected and correct for this style. Color choices reinforce the editorial mood — muted luxury tones, monochrome, or a single precise accent used intentionally. Hierarchy is supported through color use. |
| 5 | Canva Excellence | Color is a storytelling instrument. Achromatic palettes (black, white, grey, cream) are the norm; when a single accent color appears it is a precise, intentional decision — never decorative. The palette and the image exist in a continuous tonal relationship, and every color decision — including the background — contributes to the emotional temperature of the piece. Restraint is complete and purposeful. |

## 02\. Organic / Warm

*Earth-toned, handcrafted aesthetic that communicates authenticity, sustainability, and human connection. Favors natural textures, imperfect forms, and a palette derived from the natural world.*

### Rubric 1: Texture & Material Authenticity

| Score | Rating | Description |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| 1 | Broken | Digital-looking, flat, or glossy elements dominate. Any texture applied is a cheap overlay that reads as fake. The design looks like it was generated by someone who wanted to appear artisanal without understanding what that means. |
| 2 | Weak | Texture is present but inconsistently applied — some elements have it, others don't. The materials feel sampled from a stock library rather than chosen for their specific quality. The handcrafted signal is faint and undermined by polished digital elements. |
| 3 | Acceptable | Texture and material language are consistently applied across the design. The organic aesthetic is legible. However, textures may be generic (linen, kraft paper) and the execution doesn't go beyond a recognizable template. |
| 4 | Strong | Textures are specific and characterful — they feel chosen, not default. The material palette (paper grain, natural fiber, raw wood, botanical illustration, organic shapes) is coherent and reinforces the brand story. Illustration and photography may coexist, with the illustrated element softening and humanising the photographic one. Digital and analog elements are blended with skill. |
| 5 | Canva Excellence | The design makes you want to touch it. Texture is not decorative but structural — it is woven into the layout, the typography choices, and the image style. Botanical or organic illustration may serve as the entire composition, not merely a background layer — text lives on top of the illustration field as an equal participant. The organic authenticity is completely convincing and specific to the world of this brand. |

### Rubric 2: Earthy Color Palette Coherence

| Score | Rating | Description |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| 1 | Broken | Palette includes saturated, synthetic colors (bright blue, neon, cool gray) that are incompatible with organic warmth. The color choices actively contradict the intended aesthetic. |
| 2 | Weak | Warm tones are present but mixed with colors from outside the organic range. The palette is not fully committed — it hedges between warm and cool, between natural and synthetic, creating visual inconsistency. |
| 3 | Acceptable | Earth tones predominate: ochre, terracotta, sage, cream, raw umber. The palette reads as intentionally organic. However, it may rely on safe, predictable combinations rather than a distinctive point of view. |
| 4 | Strong | The palette is composed with sensitivity to temperature and saturation. Warm neutrals and muted naturals work together in a way that feels specific and considered. Color relationships create a comfortable, inviting visual atmosphere. |
| 5 | Canva Excellence | Color tells an environmental story. The palette evokes a specific natural context — a Mediterranean garden, a forest floor, a sun-dried clay studio — with such precision that it communicates mood and place instantly. Every color feels sourced from the natural world. |

### Rubric 3: Typography — Warmth & Humanity

| Score | Rating | Description |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| 1 | Broken | Cold, geometric sans-serifs or ultra-modern typefaces are used that have no relationship to the organic world. Script fonts are faux-handwritten at low quality. Typography and aesthetic are in direct conflict. |
| 2 | Weak | A slightly warmer typeface is used but not one that genuinely communicates craft or humanity. Pairing is underdeveloped. Typography is functional but contributes nothing to the warmth of the design. |
| 3 | Acceptable | Humanist sans-serif or a simple serif is used, appropriate for the organic context. Hierarchy is clear. The typography doesn't harm the aesthetic, though it doesn't elevate it either. |
| 4 | Strong | Font choices actively reinforce the organic warmth — a hand-lettered or artisanal display font for headlines, a legible humanist typeface for body. Pairing is intentional. The typography adds a layer of personality and craft. |
| 5 | Canva Excellence | Typography is expressive and authentic — it looks like it could have been hand-set or designed specifically for this brand. Imperfections, weight variation, or calligraphic details add character rather than reducing polish. Warmth is communicated through every letterform. |

### Rubric 4: Layout Rhythm & Natural Flow

| Score | Rating | Description |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| 1 | Broken | Layout is mechanical, rigid, and grid-locked. Uniform spacing and hard geometric shapes make the design feel digital and corporate. There is nothing organic about the structure — it contradicts the entire aesthetic premise. |
| 2 | Weak | Some attempt at visual softness (rounded elements, irregular spacing) but the underlying layout logic is still machine-like. The rhythm is either too uniform or too chaotic, without the natural variation that gives organic design its character. |
| 3 | Acceptable | Layout has a relaxed, readable quality. Elements are not rigidly gridded. Some asymmetry or gentle irregularity is present. The design breathes. However, the rhythm is conventional rather than distinctive. |
| 4 | Strong | The layout moves the eye naturally through the design, mimicking the irregular but harmonious patterns found in nature. Curved, flowing, or irregular shapes replace geometric containers — no hard corners or straight ruled lines. Spacing, grouping, and element sizing create a visual cadence that feels alive rather than engineered. |
| 5 | Canva Excellence | The layout itself is an expression of organic principle — imperfect, asymmetric, but deeply harmonious. It feels like something that grew into its shape rather than something that was constructed. The visual rhythm produces a genuine sense of ease and calm. |

## 03\. Bold / Expressive

*High-energy, maximalist visual language that uses oversized type, saturated color, and graphic contrast to demand immediate attention. Priority is impact, memorability, and emotional charge.*

### Rubric 1: Visual Impact & Immediate Read

| Score | Rating | Description |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| 1 | Broken | The design fails to command attention even at scale. There is no clear visual entry point. Multiple elements compete at equal weight, creating visual noise with no payoff. At thumbnail size, the design is illegible. |
| 2 | Weak | An attempt at a hero element exists but it is not bold enough to anchor the composition. The design has ambition but lacks execution — it hints at impact without delivering it. At a glance, the message is unclear. |
| 3 | Acceptable | A clear focal point exists. The design reads quickly at a normal viewing distance. The boldness is present but safe — it achieves attention without achieving excitement. The design communicates, but does not provoke. |
| 4 | Strong | The design stops the viewer. The visual hierarchy is so clear and the focal element so dominant that the message lands in under a second. Color, scale, or contrast are deployed with genuine confidence. |
| 5 | Canva Excellence | The design is visually unforgettable. It would stop a scroll, command a wall, or turn a head in a crowded space. Every decision — scale, color, placement — has been pushed to the edge of its expressive limit without tipping into incoherence. |

### Rubric 2: Color Saturation & Contrast Mastery

| Score | Rating | Description |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| 1 | Broken | Colors are garish and unrelated — multiple maximum-saturation hues fight each other with no organizing principle. There is no contrast logic: similar values sit adjacent, making the design simultaneously overwhelming and illegible. |
| 2 | Weak | Saturated colors are used but without a coherent system. The palette is aggressive without being strategic. Contrast is inconsistent — some elements are clear, others disappear into similar-value backgrounds. |
| 3 | Acceptable | A limited, high-impact palette is established. Primary or complementary color pairs create sufficient contrast. The boldness reads correctly. However, the color logic may feel borrowed from a template rather than invented for this specific message. |
| 4 | Strong | The color system is purposeful and expressive. Saturation is used as a tool — maximum intensity where the eye should land, relative calm elsewhere. Contrast ratios are strong throughout. The palette has a point of view. |
| 5 | Canva Excellence | Color is weaponized for communication. The palette creates a physical sensation — warmth, energy, urgency, joy — that is inseparable from the message. Contrast is perfect: nothing disappears, nothing wars, everything contributes to the emotional charge. |

### Rubric 3: Typographic Scale & Expressiveness

| Score | Rating | Description |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| 1 | Broken | Typography is timid — sized as if for a formal document. The typeface choice is neutral or corporate. There is no drama, no gesture, no personality. The type looks like it belongs in a different (and much quieter) design. |
| 2 | Weak | Headline is larger than body but not large enough to feel bold. The typeface may be correct in category (display, sans-serif) but is applied without conviction. Sizing relationships are conventional rather than expressive. |
| 3 | Acceptable | Type is clearly bold and appropriately scaled for the context. Display typeface fits the energetic mood. The typographic statement is coherent. Lacks the edge that would make the typography itself a statement. |
| 4 | Strong | Headline type is deployed at a scale that commands space. The typeface has genuine personality — it is heavy, unconventional, or distorted in a purposeful way. The typographic hierarchy is dramatic and clear. |
| 5 | Canva Excellence | Typography is the design. Letters are treated as graphic elements — sized to fill space, rotated, cropped, stacked, or collaged. The typographic choices are so specific and so bold that the design could not communicate its idea in any other way. Type and content are inseparable. |

### Rubric 4: Compositional Energy & Tension

| Score | Rating | Description |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| 1 | Broken | Composition is static and centered with no dynamic movement. Elements are evenly distributed across the canvas in a way that creates balance but destroys energy. The design lies flat on the page. |
| 2 | Weak | Some diagonal or asymmetric element is introduced but the underlying structure remains passive. The attempt at dynamism is timid. The design oscillates between wanting to be safe and wanting to be bold, landing on neither. |
| 3 | Acceptable | The composition has movement and a degree of tension. An off-center focal point or diagonal element introduces energy. The layout is not boring. However, the structural ambition is conventional for the bold/expressive category. |
| 4 | Strong | The composition generates visual kinetics — the eye moves through the design at speed, directed by deliberate angle, overlap, or scale contrast. Elements feel like they are in motion or in productive collision. There is genuine tension. |
| 5 | Canva Excellence | The design feels like it is about to explode off the page — or implode into itself. Compositional choices (extreme cropping, overlapping type and image, radical asymmetry, forced perspective) create a level of energy that matches the expressive ambition. The structure is unstable in a controlled, intentional way. |

## 04\. Minimalist / Clean

*Precision-driven aesthetic that communicates through reduction. Every element on the canvas must justify its presence. The goal is maximum clarity with minimum visual means — a design philosophy rooted in functional modernism.*

### Rubric 1: Element Economy & Purposeful Reduction

| Score | Rating | Description |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| 1 | Broken | Decorative elements are added that serve no structural or communicative purpose. The design calls itself minimal while including drop shadows, gradients, icon clusters, or ornamental frames that add noise without adding meaning. |
| 2 | Weak | A cleaner direction is attempted but the designer has not fully committed to removal. Some unnecessary elements remain — a divider that divides nothing, an icon that duplicates copy, a color that adds no contrast or hierarchy. |
| 3 | Acceptable | The design is genuinely minimal: a limited color palette, clean typeface, and only the necessary visual elements. It functions correctly. The reduction is real but safe — the design is minimal in the way that a blank template is minimal. |
| 4 | Strong | Every element on the canvas was chosen over an alternative and placed with precision. Nothing was left in by default. Visual elements may be photographic, geometric, or purely abstract — what matters is that each one earns its place. The reduction creates a design that communicates more through what is absent than through what is present. |
| 5 | Canva Excellence | The design achieves the paradox of minimalism: it is so stripped of the inessential that what remains feels inevitable and complete. Abstract or geometric shapes are fully valid as the sole visual element — no photography required — provided each shape serves a compositional purpose. Removing a single element would make it feel empty; adding one would ruin it. The emptiness is load-bearing. |

### Rubric 2: Grid Discipline & Spatial Precision

| Score | Rating | Description |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| 1 | Broken | Elements are placed arbitrarily with no alignment logic. Margins are inconsistent. Spacing between elements varies randomly. The lack of a grid is not expressive — it is simply careless, and in a minimalist context, this is fatal. |
| 2 | Weak | A rough alignment exists — elements are not entirely random — but the grid is not strict. Margins vary slightly, spacing is eyeballed rather than measured. In a busy design this might be invisible; in a minimal design it is the first thing noticed. |
| 3 | Acceptable | The design is clearly grid-based. Margins are consistent, elements are aligned, spacing follows a discernible logic. The structure is correct. The grid is doing its job but is not being used to create beauty or expression — only order. |
| 4 | Strong | The grid is both disciplined and expressive. Consistent spatial intervals create visual rhythm. The placement of a single element against vast negative space is clearly considered. The precision itself is part of the aesthetic experience. |
| 5 | Canva Excellence | Grid use rises to the level of spatial poetry. The position of each element within the grid has been chosen not just for alignment but for visual balance, proportion, and rhythm. The relationship between elements — the distance between them, their grouping — communicates as much as the content. |

### Rubric 3: Typography Clarity & Restraint

| Score | Rating | Description |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| 1 | Broken | Multiple typeface families are used. Weights are varied without purpose. The typographic palette is cluttered in direct contradiction of the minimalist premise. Hierarchy is unclear. |
| 2 | Weak | One typeface is used, which is correct, but weight and size variations are inconsistent or insufficient to establish clear hierarchy. The typographic system lacks the internal rigor that minimalism requires. |
| 3 | Acceptable | A single typeface family is used cleanly, with appropriate weight distinctions for hierarchy. The typography is correct and unobtrusive — it supports clarity without adding personality. |
| 4 | Strong | The typeface is chosen with care for its geometric or humanist precision. Weight contrasts are calibrated specifically for scale. Letter-spacing and leading are deliberate. The typography is a component of the visual system, not just text. |
| 5 | Canva Excellence | Typography does all the heavy lifting. In the absence of decorative elements, the font — its geometry, its stroke contrast, its proportions — becomes the primary visual interest. The typographic system is so refined that it generates visual pleasure independently of what it says. |

### Rubric 4: Negative Space as Design Element

| Score | Rating | Description |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| 1 | Broken | The designer treats empty space as wasted space and fills it instinctively. The canvas has no room to breathe. Negative space reads as neglect rather than intention. In minimalism, this is not merely a weakness — it is a fundamental misunderstanding of the form. |
| 2 | Weak | Some empty space is present but is unevenly distributed. The designer is aware of white space as a concept but is not yet confident enough to let it dominate. Large voids are filled with something small rather than left empty. |
| 3 | Acceptable | White space is generous and consistent. The canvas is not overcrowded. The design reads comfortably. Negative space is present as a functional element (improving readability) but not yet a compositional tool. |
| 4 | Strong | Negative space is actively shaped and weighted. The emptiness is distributed with the same intentionality as the content. The viewer's eye is guided not only by what is there but by the space around it. Silence is part of the score. |
| 5 | Canva Excellence | Negative space is the subject of the design. The placement of a single mark in a field of white creates visual tension, balance, and meaning that a busy design could never achieve. The designer has full command of emptiness as a medium. This is what minimalism is for. |

## 05\. Modular

*A grid-based layout system where the canvas is divided into discrete cells — like a bento box — each containing exactly one type of content: text, photograph, flat color, or color with a shape. Structure is the design. Color unifies the system. Hierarchy is built through cell proportion and reading order, not decorative emphasis.*

### Rubric 1: Cell Structure & Grid Integrity

| Score | Rating | Description |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| 1 | Broken | The cell structure is absent or collapsed. Content spills across zones with no clear boundaries between information types. Text, images, and color blocks exist in the same undefined space. The bento logic — one compartment, one content type — is completely absent. |
| 2 | Weak | Cells are implied but inconsistently defined. Some compartments hold mixed content types without reason. Borders or spacing suggest a grid was attempted but not followed through. The viewer cannot reliably tell where one cell ends and the next begins. |
| 3 | Acceptable | The cell structure is readable and functional. Each compartment holds one content type: text, photo, flat color, or color with a shape. Boundaries are clear. The system works but may be overly simple — a 2x2 grid with little variation in cell proportion or content mix. |
| 4 | Strong | The grid is well-constructed with varied cell proportions that feel considered rather than default. Content types are distributed thoughtfully — photo cells, text cells, and color-only cells balance each other across the layout. The structure itself communicates hierarchy. |
| 5 | Canva Excellence | The cell system is the design. Proportions are precise, content types are expertly distributed, and the grid creates a visual rhythm that guides the eye naturally. Every cell justifies its size and position. The structure is so well-built that changing a single cell would unbalance the whole. |

### Rubric 2: Color Palette Coherence

| Score | Rating | Description |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| 1 | Broken | Colors are chosen randomly with no relationship to each other. A saturated red sits next to a pastel lavender next to a neon yellow — the palette has no internal logic. The color system actively works against the grid by making cells feel disconnected rather than part of a unified design. |
| 2 | Weak | A partial palette exists but is undermined by one or more colors that do not belong to the same family or saturation level. The inconsistency is visually jarring. The palette feels like it was assembled from different templates rather than chosen as a coherent set. |
| 3 | Acceptable | The palette is coherent — colors share a tonal register (all vibrant, all pastel, all muted) and do not clash. The combination is safe and functional. The palette works but may rely on an obvious pairing rather than a considered one specific to the content. |
| 4 | Strong | The palette is curated with clear intention. Colors are from the same family and saturation tier, and they are distributed across cells in a way that creates visual balance. No single cell dominates incorrectly due to color weight. The palette feels chosen, not defaulted. |
| 5 | Canva Excellence | The color system is so well-calibrated that it does double duty: it unifies the design visually and reinforces the information structure. The palette is specific and memorable — it could be lifted and applied to a brand identity. Every color earns its place in the grid. |

### Rubric 3: Content Breathing Room

| Score | Rating | Description |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| 1 | Broken | Text is cramped against cell edges with no internal margin. Letters touch or nearly touch the cell border. In some cells text overflows its container. The content feels imprisoned rather than housed. Reading is uncomfortable and the design looks unfinished. |
| 2 | Weak | Some padding exists but is uneven — text may have space on one side and none on another. Smaller cells are over-filled with copy that should have been reduced or broken across cells. The breathing room is inconsistent enough to feel like an oversight rather than a choice. |
| 3 | Acceptable | Text sits comfortably inside each cell with adequate and roughly consistent padding. No text touches a border. Content is appropriately sized for its cell. The spacing is correct but uniform — it meets the standard without using space as a design tool. |
| 4 | Strong | Padding is generous and consistent across all cells. Text scale is calibrated to cell size — larger cells have larger type, smaller cells have appropriately reduced copy. The white space inside each cell feels intentional, giving content room to breathe and making each cell pleasant to read. |
| 5 | Canva Excellence | The internal spacing of each cell is a micro-design decision. Padding, type size, and content amount are in perfect relationship with the cell's dimensions. The text never fights its container — it anchors within it. Every cell, regardless of size, feels like it was designed individually and then assembled into the grid. |

### Rubric 4: Reading Flow & Information Hierarchy

| Score | Rating | Description |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| 1 | Broken | There is no readable hierarchy. The most important information is not visually dominant. Secondary details compete at the same weight. The eye has no entry point and no guided path through the layout. The design must be decoded, not read. |
| 2 | Weak | A focal point exists but the reading path breaks down quickly. After the headline, the eye jumps randomly between cells of similar visual weight. Left-to-right, top-to-bottom reading logic is not supported. Important information is buried in lower or peripheral cells. |
| 3 | Acceptable | The primary information is clearly the largest or most prominent element. The reading flow is broadly left-to-right and top-to-bottom. Most viewers would extract the key message quickly. However the hierarchy among secondary information is flat — everything below the headline feels equally important. |
| 4 | Strong | Hierarchy is clear from primary to secondary to tertiary information. The eye moves through the grid in a logical sequence driven by cell size, type scale, and color weight. The most critical information is encountered first and the least critical last. The layout rewards a full read. |
| 5 | Canva Excellence | The grid is a perfectly engineered reading machine. From the first cell to the last, the viewer is guided through information in exact order of importance with no ambiguity. Cell size, type scale, color, and position all work in concert to create a hierarchy so clear it feels inevitable. The design communicates its full message in a single, effortless scan. |

## Universal Rubric — Prompt Fidelity & Instructional Accuracy

*Applied independently of design style. Evaluates how faithfully the AI-generated design translates the original user prompt into a visual outcome. A design can be technically beautiful and still score a 1 here if it ignores the brief.*

**Special rule:** If the prompt explicitly names a design style and the delivered design does not match that style, the maximum score is **2**, regardless of other qualities.

| Score | Rating | Description |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| 1 | Off-Brief | The design bears no meaningful relationship to the prompt. Core subject matter is wrong, the requested format or use case is ignored, and any specific instructions have been disregarded entirely. The output could have been generated from a completely different brief. Unusable without a full rebuild. |
| 2 | Partial Match | The design captures the general topic or category of the prompt but misses critical specifics. The subject is correct but requested mood, format, copy, or style is wrong. One or two key prompt elements are present, but enough is missing that the design would not serve its intended purpose without significant revision. |
| 3 | Mostly Aligned | The design addresses the main subject and intent of the prompt. Format, audience, and general visual direction are correct. However, one or more secondary instructions are missing or interpreted loosely. The design is usable but not fully faithful. |
| 4 | Faithful | All primary and most secondary prompt instructions are reflected in the design. Subject, format, copy, mood, and style direction are correctly interpreted. Any deviation is minor — a reasonable creative decision rather than an oversight. Only very granular details might be missing or approximated. |
| 5 | Canva Excellence | Every element of the prompt is accounted for and translated into a design decision. Subject matter, copy, visual style, format, dimensions, audience, tone, and any named references are all present and correctly interpreted. Where the prompt left room for creative interpretation, the choices made enhance rather than contradict the stated intent. The design is a complete, accurate, and intelligent response to the brief — it could be delivered to a client without explanation. |
